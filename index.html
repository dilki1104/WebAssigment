02

History

Sri Lanka carries a heritage and a success story for their tea like non other. About two hundred years ago, when the country was still a British colony, the tale of Ceylon tea starts. In 1824, the British brought in a tea plant from China to Ceylon and cultivated it for non-commercial purposes in the Royal Botanical Gardens in Peradeniya. The Scotsman, James Taylor, introduced commercial tea cultivation in Sri Lanka when he arrived in 1852. He arrived and settled on the estate of Loolecondera in Kandy. In 1872, Taylor started a fully equipped tea factory in the Loolecondera estate and in 1875 he managed to send the first shipment of Ceylon tea to the London tea auction, which made it more famous.

03

Global Market

From 2012-2014 we saw a revival in Ceylon Tea, with the year 2013 smashing all records and the 2014 figures just released surpassing even that of 2013, showing the highest export earnings ever of $1.61 billion (Rs. 210.2 billion), which is a 5.43 percent increase over 2013 and a FOB price of US$ 4.95 per kg, which is a 3.1 percent increase over 2013, all records for 2013. Growth in export earnings from value-added tea products such as Tea Bags (118.6 percent), Green Tea (49 percent) and Tea Packets (9.24) was led by the positive output of tea exports during 2014. Export earnings from Tea Bags contributed 48.5 percent of total tea exports. Sri Lankan tea exports reached the number 2 spot among global tea exporters by mid-2014 with these high export volumes and prices, surpassing China.
